# Consistency Checklist

Use this guide for theses assembled from multiple papers or chapters written at different times.

## Project Style Profile

Derive these from the current thesis before proposing changes:

- Heading style: title case vs sentence case, singular/plural section names, numeric-experiment naming.
- Chapter pattern: background, contributions, organization, notation, summary, conclusion, future work, technical details.
- Cross-reference style: `\ref`, `\eqref`, `\cref`, `\autoref`, hard-coded numbers.
- Citation style: author-year vs numeric, citation placement, first-occurrence citations.
- Term style: hyphenation, capitalization, acronym expansion, singular/plural forms, translation pairs.
- Notation style: symbol definitions, reused symbols, bold/calligraphic conventions, index conventions.

## Cross-Chapter Checks

- Ensure each chapter's opening and closing sections serve comparable functions or intentionally differ.
- Remove or soften repeated paper-introduction boilerplate when it disrupts thesis flow.
- Check whether each chapter redefines terms or notation already defined in preliminaries.
- Check transitions between chapters and the global introduction/conclusion for consistent contribution claims.
- Ensure acronyms are expanded on first use in the thesis body or in each chapter, depending on local convention.

## Language and Style

- Prefer precise academic prose over promotional phrasing.
- Avoid unnecessary coined compounds; keep normal noun phrases unhyphenated unless used as compound modifiers or established terms.
- Keep terminology stable across title, abstract, keywords, chapter headings, captions, and bibliography.
- Check common residue from paper merging: "this paper" vs "this thesis", inconsistent "we propose/show/study", duplicated literature summaries, and mismatched tense.

## Abstract and Keywords

- Ensure the abstract states problem setting, methods, main contributions, and limitations at a comparable level of detail in all languages.
- Check that bilingual abstracts are meaning-equivalent, not word-for-word translations with missing claims.
- Align keywords in count, order, terminology, capitalization, and acronym usage.

## Output

Classify findings:

- `Must fix`: visible errors, grammar defects, wrong references, inconsistent numbering, broken PDF/TOC, examiner-required changes.
- `Should fix`: style inconsistencies that reduce polish but do not change meaning.
- `Leave/confirm`: changes that depend on author preference, advisor convention, or school template rules.
